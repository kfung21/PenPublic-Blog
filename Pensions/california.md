# California Pension Systems

California has **over 80 public retirement systems**, making it the most complex pension landscape of any U.S. state. These systems collectively cover more than <a href="https://www.ppic.org/publication/public-pensions-in-california/">4.6 million members</a> — roughly one in nine Californians — and hold <a href="https://www.sco.ca.gov/eo_investments_and_pensions.html">combined assets exceeding $800 billion</a>.

The systems break down into three tiers: statewide systems, county-level systems (governed by the 1937 Act), and independent city/agency systems. A web of reciprocal agreements connects many of these systems, allowing workers to move between public employers without losing retirement benefits.

* see also: <a href="https://www.nasra.org/ca">NASRA — California Pension Profile</a>

## The Three Statewide Systems

### 1. California Public Employees' Retirement System (CalPERS)

**The largest public pension fund in the United States.**

- **Members:** 1.5+ million (employees, retirees, and their families)
- **Assets:** ~$556 billion (FY 2024-25)
- **Coverage:** State employees, 37 counties, 1,500+ cities, special districts, and school classified staff
- **Governing law:** <a href="https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayexpandedbranch.xhtml?tocCode=GOV&division=5.&title=2.&part=3.&chapter=&article=">Public Employees' Retirement Law (PERL), California Government Code §20000 et seq.</a>
- **Board:** 13-member Board of Administration

CalPERS provides defined benefit pension plans, health benefits, and supplemental income plans (457). Its membership falls into two broad categories: **Miscellaneous** (non-hazardous occupations) and **Safety** (law enforcement, fire, corrections).

* see also: <a href="https://www.calpers.ca.gov/">CalPERS Official Site</a>
* see also: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CalPERS">CalPERS Wikipedia</a>

### 2. California State Teachers' Retirement System (CalSTRS)

**The largest educator-only pension fund in the world.**

- **Members:** ~975,000+
- **Coverage:** Certificated public school teachers (K-12) and community college instructors
- **Governing law:** <a href="https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayexpandedbranch.xhtml?tocCode=EDC&division=&title=1.&part=13.&chapter=&article=">Teachers' Retirement Law, California Education Code Part 13, §22000 et seq.</a>
- **Board:** 12-member Teachers' Retirement Board (CA Education Code §22200)

CalSTRS operates three programs:
- **Defined Benefit (DB) Program** — the core pension
- **Defined Benefit Supplement (DBS) Program** — a cash balance plan that accumulates from extra-duty pay and employer contributions
- **CalSTRS Pension2** — voluntary 403(b) and 457(b) savings plans

* see also: <a href="https://www.calstrs.com/">CalSTRS Official Site</a>
* see also: <a href="https://www.calstrs.com/two-benefit-structures">CalSTRS Two Benefit Structures</a>

### 3. University of California Retirement System (UCRS)

- **Coverage:** University of California employees across all 10 campuses, 5 medical centers, and affiliated research facilities
- **Core plan:** UC Retirement Plan (UCRP) — a defined benefit plan
- **Supplemental:** UC 403(b), 457(b), and DC plans
- **Reciprocity:** <a href="https://www.erso.berkeley.edu/web/sites/default/documents/Reciprocityfact.pdf">UCRP has a reciprocal agreement with CalPERS</a> (DB plan only; cash balance plans are not eligible)

* see also: <a href="https://ucnet.universityofcalifornia.edu/resources/employment-policies-contracts/bargaining-units/">UCnet Bargaining Units</a>
* see also: <a href="https://ucnet.universityofcalifornia.edu/benefits/retirement/uc-retirement-choice-program/">UC Retirement Choice Program</a>

## The 1937 Act County Systems (CERL)

The <a href="https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayexpandedbranch.xhtml?tocCode=GOV&division=4.&title=3.&part=3.&chapter=3.&article=">County Employees Retirement Law of 1937 (CERL)</a>, codified at California Government Code §31450 et seq., authorized individual counties to establish their own independent retirement systems. <a href="https://www.acera.org/acera-law">Twenty counties</a> currently operate under this act, administered collectively through the <a href="https://sacrs.org/Resources/County-Employees-Retirement-Law-CERL">State Association of County Retirement Systems (SACRS)</a>.

Each 1937 Act system is an independent entity with its own Board of Retirement, investment portfolio, and benefit structures — but all are governed by the same underlying CERL statute plus the California Constitution Article XVI, Section 17, and PEPRA.

## Reciprocity — How the Systems Connect

<a href="https://www.calpers.ca.gov/members/retirement-benefits/reciprocity">Reciprocity</a> is a formal agreement among California public retirement systems that allows members to move between systems without losing certain benefits. It does NOT transfer money or service credit between systems — instead, it coordinates benefits at retirement.

### How Reciprocity Works

1. You leave one public retirement system and join another **within 180 days** (6 months)
2. You leave your contributions on deposit with the old system
3. At retirement, you retire from **both** systems on the **same date**
4. Each system calculates your benefit independently, but the **highest final compensation** from either system can be used in both calculations
5. Service credit from both systems can be combined to meet **vesting requirements** (typically 5 years)

### Systems with CalPERS Reciprocal Agreements

The <a href="https://www.calpers.ca.gov/documents/member-reciprocal-self-certification-form-calpers-1187/download?inline=">CalPERS Reciprocal Self-Certification Form (CalPERS-1187)</a> identifies the following qualifying systems:

- California State Teachers' Retirement System (CalSTRS) — DB plan only
- All 20 CERL/1937 Act county systems
- Los Angeles City Employees' Retirement System (LACERS) — Miscellaneous only
- Sacramento County Employees' Retirement System (SCERS) — DB plan only
- San Diego City Employees' Retirement System (SDCERS) — DB plan only
- University of California Retirement Program (UCRP) — DB plan only
- Judges' Retirement System (JRS / JRS II)
- Legislators' Retirement System (LRS)

### NOT Reciprocal with CalPERS

- L.A. Fire and Police Pension System (LAFPP)
- L.A. Water and Power Employees' Retirement System (LAWPERS)
- Federal Employee Retirement System (FERS)

<a href="https://mysfers.org/active-members/reciprocity/">SFERS</a> maintains its own reciprocal agreements that generally mirror CalPERS reciprocity, with the notable exception that SFERS does **not** have reciprocity with UCRP, JRS, LRS, or CalSTRS.

## How We Got Here — The Road from SB 400 to PEPRA (1999–2012)

The story of California's pension landscape cannot be understood without understanding the decade-plus arc from the dot-com boom to the Great Recession and the political crisis that followed. California was not alone — **48 of 50 states** enacted major pension reform after 2008 — but the state's uniquely broken budget structure made the crisis especially severe.

### California's Structural Budget Problem

California's chronic budget instability traces back to <a href="https://time.com/archive/6688982/spotlight-californias-budget-crisis/">Proposition 13 (1978)</a>, which capped property taxes at 1% of assessed value and forced the state to depend on income and capital gains taxes for revenue. By the 2000s, <a href="https://www.governing.com/finance/can-california-ever-escape-its-boom-and-bust-budget-woes">personal income tax made up nearly 70% of general fund revenue</a> — compared to 38% nationally. This created a boom-bust cycle: when the stock market surged, California was flush. When it crashed, the floor dropped out.

The <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_budget_crisis">dot-com bust (2001-2003)</a> blew a $38 billion hole in the budget — the largest of any state in the country. Governor Gray Davis tried to close it with a vehicle registration fee increase, was recalled, and Arnold Schwarzenegger came in and immediately reversed the fee. <a href="https://lao.ca.gov/Publications/Report/5101">The state responded with mostly short-term solutions rather than fixing the structural imbalance</a>, so when the Great Recession hit just a few years later, California entered it with a structural deficit and virtually no reserves.

### The Boom: SB 400 and the Enhancement Era (1999–2006)

In the late 1990s, CalPERS was riding high. A roaring stock market had pushed investment returns to an average of 13.5% per year over the prior decade, with 20% returns in 1997 and 1998. <a href="https://californiapolicycenter.org/repeat-pension-history/">CalPERS achieved a funded ratio of 120.5%</a> — meaning it had more money than it needed to pay all projected future benefits. There was a literal surplus.

Rather than banking the surplus against future downturns, CalPERS itself sponsored legislation to enhance benefits. <a href="https://capitolweekly.net/calpers-sponsored-bill-increased-pensions/">Senate Bill 400</a>, authored by Sen. Deborah Ortiz (D-Sacramento) and signed by Governor Gray Davis on September 29, 1999, made sweeping changes:

- **Created the 3% @ 50 formula** for Highway Patrol officers, up from 2% @ 50 — a roughly 50% increase in benefits
- **Authorized local police and fire agencies** to adopt the same 3% @ 50 formula through their own CalPERS contracts
- **Upgraded state miscellaneous employees** from 2% @ 60 to 2% @ 55
- **Applied retroactively** — the new, higher multipliers applied to all past years of service, not just future years
- **Changed final compensation** for school members from 36-month to 12-month averaging, making pension spiking easier

CalPERS told the Legislature the enhancements would not cost taxpayers additional money. <a href="https://capitolweekly.net/calpers-sponsored-bill-increased-pensions/">CalPERS President William Crist said SB 400 would not cost "a dime of additional taxpayer money."</a> The assumption: continued strong market returns would cover everything.

The math told a different story almost immediately. State contributions to CalPERS, which had dropped from $1.2 billion in FY 1997 to just $160 million in FY 1999-2000, began soaring after the dot-com bubble burst in 2000-2001.

SB 400 triggered a statewide **"arms race"** of pension enhancements. Local agencies rushed to adopt 3% @ 50 for safety employees and 2.7% @ 55 or 3% @ 60 for miscellaneous employees, often arguing they had to match neighboring jurisdictions to retain workers. In 2001, <a href="https://www.comstocksmag.com/longread/untying-knot">Assembly Bill 616 allowed local miscellaneous employees to receive 3% @ 60</a>, further escalating the bidding war. By the mid-2000s, many of the generous formulas that would later be called "unsustainable" were locked into CalPERS contracts across the state.

* see also: <a href="http://www.leginfo.ca.gov/pub/99-00/bill/sen/sb_0351-0400/sb_400_bill_19990929_chaptered.html">SB 400 Full Bill Text</a>
* see also: <a href="https://calpensions.com/category/calpers/sb-400/">CalPensions — SB 400 Archive</a>

### Schwarzenegger's Failed Reform Attempt (2005–2010)

Governor Schwarzenegger recognized the pension problem early. In his 2005 State of the State address, he called the state's pension obligations a "financial train on another track to disaster" and <a href="https://www.labornotes.org/2006/02/california-nurses-lead-fight-against-arnold-schwarzeneggers-anti-union-ballot-measures">proposed privatizing the state's pension system</a> by shifting new employees to 401(k)-style plans.

The proposal was a political catastrophe. It would have inadvertently eliminated survivor benefits for police and firefighters killed on the job, uniting every public safety union against the Governor. Schwarzenegger dropped the pension initiative but pressed ahead with a November 2005 special election featuring four reform propositions targeting union dues, teacher tenure, spending caps, and redistricting. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2005_California_special_election">Public employee unions spent over $100 million fighting the measures</a> — the California Teachers Association alone spent $56 million and mortgaged its headquarters. All four propositions were crushed, and Schwarzenegger's approval ratings hit record lows.

Arnold didn't entirely give up. <a href="https://www.schwarzenegger.com/issues/milestone/reforming-our-pension-system">In 2010, a Stanford University study he commissioned revealed $500 billion in unfunded pension debt</a> — roughly ten times what CalPERS was officially reporting. In his final year, he negotiated pension rollbacks with several individual unions through bargaining, moving CHP officers and firefighters back to pre-SB 400 benefit levels. But comprehensive legislative reform eluded him.

### The Great Recession (2008–2009)

The 2008 financial crisis devastated CalPERS. The fund lost approximately **$100 billion** in value during the stock market crash, and its funded ratio plummeted. Real estate investments were particularly catastrophic — <a href="https://www.city-journal.org/article/the-pension-fund-that-ate-california">CalPERS had pumped $7 billion into real estate between 2004 and 2006</a>, owning 288,000 homes and lots, 80% of them in bubble states (California, Florida, Arizona).

Meanwhile, the state budget was in freefall. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_budget_crisis">Revenues dropped from over $100 billion in 2007 to about $85 billion in 2008</a>, and a two-thirds supermajority requirement to pass budgets or raise taxes meant gridlock. California started issuing IOUs in July 2009 because it literally could not pay its bills.

The crash exposed several compounding problems:

- **Pension enhancements from SB 400 era** were now permanent obligations, locked in by the "California Rule" (the judicial doctrine that pension benefits, once granted, generally cannot be reduced)
- **Unfunded liabilities ballooned** as asset values cratered but benefit obligations remained
- **Employer contribution rates** began a long-delayed series of increases, eventually doubling annual state and local government pension costs
- **CalPERS delayed rate increases** for four years (2009–2012), using smoothing techniques that allowed debt to grow even further

### A National Crisis, Not Just California

California was far from alone. The Great Recession triggered pension reform across essentially the entire country. <a href="https://www.nirsonline.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/pensionreform.pdf">Since 2008, 48 of 50 states passed major pension reform.</a> The <a href="https://www.nationalaffairs.com/publications/detail/how-congress-can-help-state-pension-reform">pace accelerated rapidly: 10 states enacted reforms in 2009, 21 in 2010, and 32 in 2011.</a>

Some notable examples:

- **Rhode Island (2011):** Enacted the most aggressive reform in the country under Treasurer Gina Raimondo. <a href="https://www.civicfed.org/iifs/blog/what-are-rhode-island-pension-reforms">Froze existing employees' accrued benefits and moved them to a hybrid plan with just 1% per year of service plus a 401(k)-style defined contribution account.</a> Rhode Island's funded ratio had fallen to just 48.4%.
- **Wisconsin (2011):** <a href="https://alec.org/article/sound-pension-reform-helps-states-keep-the-promise/">Governor Scott Walker's reforms required public employees to contribute half of their annual pension contribution</a>, sparking mass protests and a recall election. Wisconsin went on to become the best-funded pension system in the country.
- **New Jersey (2011):** Had the third-largest unfunded liability in the nation at $34.4 billion. Passed reforms increasing employee contributions and reducing COLAs.
- **New York (2012):** Created a new Tier 6 for employees hired after April 2012, with a lower benefit multiplier and higher retirement age, similar in concept to California's PEPRA.

The pattern was the same everywhere: the Great Recession cratered pension fund investments at the same time state revenues collapsed, creating a double squeeze. States that had enhanced benefits during the boom years were hit hardest.

### The Scandals and Bankruptcies (2010–2012)

Public outrage over pension costs intensified as several high-profile events converged:

- **The Bell Scandal (2010):** The city of Bell, California became a national symbol of government compensation abuse when it was revealed that <a href="https://reason.org/commentary/bell-california-government-salary-p/">City Manager Robert Rizzo was receiving nearly $800,000 in salary</a> and a total compensation package exceeding $1.5 million, putting taxpayers on the hook for over $600,000 per year in pension costs for life.

- **Municipal Bankruptcies:** Three California cities filed for Chapter 9 bankruptcy in rapid succession — <a href="https://gspp.berkeley.edu/research-and-impact/news/recent-news/hidden-crisis-americas-public-pension-system">Vallejo (2008), Stockton (2012), and San Bernardino (2012)</a> — each citing retirement costs as a significant factor. Stockton's pension contributions had risen from $6.8 million in 2002 to $41.5 million by 2017.

- **Pension Spiking:** Media investigations exposed widespread practices where employees inflated their final-year compensation through overtime hoarding, unused vacation cashouts, and special pay to boost their lifetime pension payouts.

- **Public Opinion Shifted:** A <a href="https://calpensions.com/2020/01/08/calpensions-wrap-up-a-look-back-at-the-crisis/">PPIC survey found that 83% of Californians viewed pension spending as a problem in December 2011</a>, with 47% calling it a "big problem."

- **Local Reform Efforts:** San Diego and San Jose both placed pension reform measures on the ballot in 2012. San Diego's Proposition B passed with strong support, switching new hires to 401(k)-style plans. San Jose's Measure B passed with 70% support but was later gutted by the courts.

### Governor Brown's 12-Point Plan and the Passage of PEPRA (2012)

Governor Jerry Brown, despite his long alliance with public employee unions, recognized the political and fiscal reality. In October 2011, he proposed a <a href="https://saccountyretirees.com/pension-reform/associated-press-highlights-of-californias-pension-history/">12-point pension reform plan</a> that would have raised the retirement age to 67 for new non-safety employees and required equal cost-sharing.

The path to legislation was rocky. Senate Democrats initially blocked reform bills, and Senate Republicans accused them of refusing to hear debate. <a href="https://reason.org/wp-content/uploads/files/pepra_pension_reform.pdf">The Senate Conference Committee on Pensions refused to hear the plans</a>, and the deadline to place pension reform on the 2012 ballot expired without action.

Then, in the final days of the 2012 legislative session, <a href="https://sloansakai.com/public-law-e-alert-california-legislature-passes-pension-reform-bill-ab-340/">Assembly Bill 340</a>, sponsored by Assemblyman Warren Furutani (D-Lakewood), emerged from conference committee with the Governor's support. The bill was written and passed with extraordinary speed — <a href="https://reason.org/wp-content/uploads/files/pepra_pension_reform.pdf">the legally required 72-hour public review window never happened</a>, and the language was passed without actuarial analysis.

Key dates:
- **August 31, 2012** — Legislature adopted AB 340
- **September 12, 2012** — <a href="https://archive.gov.ca.gov/archive/gov39/2012/09/12/news17720/index.html">Governor Brown signed AB 340 and companion bill AB 197</a>
- **January 1, 2013** — PEPRA took effect

Governor Brown called it <a href="https://calmatters.org/economy/2019/03/california-pension-public-employees-jerry-brown-airtime/">"the biggest rollback to public pension benefits in the history of California."</a> It was a remarkable political achievement — <a href="https://calmatters.org/commentary/2025/05/public-pension-reform-california/">a Democrat-dominated Legislature passed reform that was universally opposed by public employee unions</a>, the Capitol's most powerful political interest group. That same year, voters passed Proposition 30 (a temporary income tax increase on high earners) to fix the revenue side. Together, PEPRA and Prop 30 finally broke the cycle of deficits that had plagued the state since 2001.

The irony: <a href="https://www.foxandhoundsdaily.com/2018/03/setting-record-straight-calpers-sb-400/">Gray Davis himself said in 2016</a> that if he knew then what he knows now, he would not have signed SB 400.

### The Aftermath and Ongoing Challenges

PEPRA's reforms, while significant, primarily affect **new hires** — not existing employees. <a href="https://reason.org/commentary/undoing-public-pension-reforms-would-cost-california-taxpayers/">CalPERS estimates the law has saved $5.8 billion so far, with $26.5 billion in additional savings projected over the next decade.</a> But the legacy liabilities from the SB 400 era remain enormous.

As of 2025, <a href="https://news.calpers.ca.gov/pepra-explained-addressing-the-top-questions-from-members/">nearly 68% of CalPERS active members are now PEPRA members</a>, projected to reach 90% within the next decade. The system is slowly transitioning, but unfunded liabilities persist.

In 2025, new legislation (AB 1383, AB 569, SB 443) has been introduced to <a href="https://calmatters.org/commentary/2025/05/public-pension-reform-california/">roll back key PEPRA provisions</a>, particularly for public safety recruitment and retention. The debate over whether California's pension system is sustainable — or whether it will repeat the SB 400 cycle — remains very much alive.

## PEPRA — The 2013 Pension Reform Act

PEPRA (AB 340), signed September 12, 2012, capped formulas for new members at **2% @ 62** (miscellaneous) and **2.7% @ 57** (safety), mandated 36-month final compensation averaging, required employees to pay 50% of normal cost, and banned retroactive benefit enhancements.

## Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs) — How Contracts Work

### What Is an MOU?

A **Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)** is the labor contract produced through collective bargaining. It spells out the terms and conditions of employment for a bargaining unit, including salary, benefits, working conditions, leave policies, grievance procedures, and retirement-related provisions. MOUs typically cover 1-3 year periods.

### The MOU Lifecycle (State Level)

1. **Sunshine Meeting:** Before negotiations begin, <a href="https://www.calhr.ca.gov/about-calhr/divisions-programs/labor-relations/bargaining-contracts/">CalHR and the union release initial bargaining proposals at a public meeting</a>, as required by the Dills Act. The public can comment.
2. **Bargaining:** CalHR's management team and the union's negotiating team meet privately to exchange proposals and negotiate.
3. **Tentative Agreement:** When both sides agree, the result is a "tentative agreement."
4. **Union Ratification:** The union presents the tentative agreement to its members for a vote.
5. **Legislative Approval:** The Legislature must vote on the economic provisions and any required law changes (<a href="https://lao.ca.gov/stateworkforce">since 2005, the LAO prepares fiscal analyses of proposed MOUs</a>).
6. **Governor's Signature:** After legislative approval, the Governor signs the MOU into effect.
7. **Continuation:** When an MOU expires, its provisions typically continue in effect until a new MOU is ratified.

### What MOUs Cover Regarding Pensions

While the pension **formula** itself is set by statute (Government Code and PEPRA) and the **employer's contract with CalPERS** (not the MOU directly), MOUs can and do address:

- **Employee contribution rates** — particularly whether the employer will pick up ("employer paid member contributions" or EPMC) a portion of the employee's required contribution, or whether employees pay above the statutory minimum
- **Retirement benefit tiers** — MOUs may reference or trigger contract amendments with CalPERS for different tiers
- **Employer contribution cost-sharing** — negotiating who bears the cost of increasing pension obligations
- **Supplemental retirement plans** — 457(b), 401(a), or other deferred compensation provisions
- **Retiree health benefits** — vesting schedules for post-retirement health coverage
- **Service credit purchase provisions** — special terms for buybacks

## Local Government Bargaining — The Meyers-Milias-Brown Act

### Overview

The <a href="https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayText.xhtml?lawCode=GOV&division=4.&title=1.&chapter=10.">Meyers-Milias-Brown Act (MMBA)</a>, codified at California Government Code §3500-3511, governs labor relations for **all local public agencies** in California — cities, counties, and most special districts. Passed in 1968, it was the last major California public-sector collective bargaining law to be enacted.

### How the MMBA Works

The MMBA requires local public employers to <a href="https://capeunion.org/contracts/meyers-milias-brown-act">"meet and confer in good faith"</a> with recognized employee organizations on wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment. Key features:

- Unlike the Dills Act (state level), the MMBA was originally enforced through the courts, not an administrative agency. In 2001, the <a href="https://perb.ca.gov/laws-and-regulations/">Public Employment Relations Board (PERB)</a> was given jurisdiction over MMBA disputes.
- The employer cannot make **unilateral changes** to wages or working conditions until bargaining has reached impasse.
- All personnel-related topics are generally subject to negotiations, except where management has a compelling operational need.
- <a href="https://sbcdsa.org/2018/09/27/the-meyers-milias-brown-act-county-city-employees-bargaining-law/">MOUs produced under the MMBA are binding contracts</a> — this was not always clear in the law's early years but has been firmly established through case law.

### Impasse Resolution Under MMBA

If negotiations reach impasse, the process follows PERB procedures:
1. **Mediation** — a neutral mediator assists negotiations
2. **Factfinding** — a factfinding panel issues non-binding recommendations
3. **Legislative body action** — the city council or board of supervisors can impose terms as a last resort

<a href="https://calattorneys.org/members/new-mou/">There is no mechanism for binding arbitration under California public-sector labor law</a> unless both parties voluntarily agree to it.

For how pension formulas are selected and changed at the local level, see **[California Pension Formulas → How Formulas Get Determined](./california-formulas#how-pension-formulas-get-determined-at-the-local-level)**.

## Key Pension Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Classic Member** | A CalPERS/CalSTRS member who first joined before 1/1/2013 and has maintained continuous membership or reciprocity |
| **PEPRA Member** | A "new member" who first joined a California public system on or after 1/1/2013 without reciprocity |
| **Benefit Factor / Age Factor** | The percentage of final compensation earned per year of service, which increases with retirement age |
| **Final Compensation** | Highest average annual salary over 12 or 36 consecutive months (depending on formula and hire date) |
| **Service Credit** | Total years and partial years of qualifying employment |
| **Normal Cost** | The annual cost of pension benefits earned during the current year |
| **Unfunded Accrued Liability (UAL)** | The difference between promised benefits and current assets |
| **COLA** | Cost-of-living adjustment — <a href="https://legalclarity.org/how-does-calpers-work-pension-benefits-explained/">typically 2% annual cap for most CalPERS contracts</a> |
| **EPMC** | Employer Paid Member Contributions — when the employer pays part of the employee's required contribution |
| **Vesting** | Achieving eligibility for a pension benefit — typically 5 years of service credit |
| **Reciprocity** | Agreement between California public systems allowing coordinated benefits when moving between systems |
| **Sunshine Meeting** | Required public meeting before bargaining begins where initial proposals are disclosed |
| **Impasse** | When bargaining parties cannot reach agreement after good-faith negotiations |
| **COLA Bank** | Accumulated unused COLA when inflation is below the contracted cap, applied in future high-inflation years |
| **MOU** | Memorandum of Understanding — the labor contract between employer and bargaining unit |
| **PERL** | Public Employees' Retirement Law — the statute governing CalPERS |
| **CERL** | County Employees Retirement Law of 1937 — the statute governing the 20 county systems |

## Key Legal and Constitutional Foundations

| Authority | What It Does |
|-----------|-------------|
| **CA Constitution, Article XVI, §17** | Grants pension boards plenary authority and fiduciary responsibility; benefits to members take precedence over all other duties |
| **Proposition 162 (1992)** | The "California Pension Protection Act" — amended Article XVI §17 to give boards sole fiduciary control |
| **PERL** (Gov. Code §20000+) | The Public Employees' Retirement Law — governs CalPERS |
| **CERL** (Gov. Code §31450+) | The County Employees Retirement Law of 1937 — governs the 20 county systems |
| **Education Code Part 13** (§22000+) | The Teachers' Retirement Law — governs CalSTRS |
| **PEPRA** (Gov. Code §7522+) | The Public Employees' Pension Reform Act of 2013 — reform law applying to all systems |
| **Dills Act** (Gov. Code §3512+) | State employee collective bargaining |
| **MMBA** (Gov. Code §3500+) | Local government collective bargaining |
| **California Rule** | Judicial doctrine holding that pension benefits constitute a contractual obligation that generally cannot be reduced for service already earned |
