# D.C. & Federal Pension Systems

Washington, D.C. is the epicenter of American public employment. The federal government alone employs over 2.8 million civilian workers, and the District of Columbia government adds tens of thousands more — police, firefighters, teachers, and general government employees. Unlike any state, D.C. sits at the intersection of two completely separate pension universes: the **federal civil service systems** administered by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), and the **D.C. local government systems** administered by the District of Columbia Retirement Board (DCRB).

This guide covers every retirement system you'll encounter working for the federal government or the District of Columbia, how bargaining units operate, and how pension formulas are determined.

## The Federal Retirement Systems

The federal government operates several distinct retirement systems. Which one covers you depends on when you were hired, which agency you work for, and whether you hold a special-category position.

### System Overview

| System | Administered By | Covers | Status |
|--------|----------------|--------|--------|
| **FERS** (Federal Employees Retirement System) | <a href="https://www.opm.gov/retirement-center/fers-information/">OPM</a> | Most civilian federal employees hired after 12/31/1983 | Active — covers ~98% of current workforce |
| **CSRS** (Civil Service Retirement System) | <a href="https://www.opm.gov/retirement-center/csrs-information/">OPM</a> | Employees hired before 1/1/1984 who did not convert to FERS | Closed to new entrants since 1984 |
| **CSRS Offset** | <a href="https://www.opm.gov/retirement-center/csrs-information/">OPM</a> | Employees rehired after 12/31/1983 with break in CSRS coverage >1 year but ≥5 years prior CSRS service | Closed — transitional category |
| **FSRDS** (Foreign Service Retirement & Disability System) | <a href="https://fam.state.gov/fam/03fam/03fam6110.html">State Department (GTM/RET)</a> | Foreign Service Officers hired before 1984 | Closed to new entrants |
| **FSPS** (Foreign Service Pension System) | <a href="https://fam.state.gov/fam/03fam/03fam6110.html">State Department (GTM/RET)</a> | Foreign Service Officers hired after 12/31/1983 | Active |
| **CIARDS** (CIA Retirement & Disability System) | CIA | Certain CIA special-category employees | <a href="https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R47084.html">Closed to new entrants since 1984</a> |
| **Federal Reserve System (FRS)** | <a href="https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R47084.html">Federal Reserve Board</a> | Fed Reserve Bank employees, Board of Governors, CFPB | Active — operates independently; no employee contributions required |
| **TSP** (Thrift Savings Plan) | <a href="https://www.tsp.gov">Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board</a> | All FERS/CSRS/FSPS employees (matching for FERS/FSPS only) | Active — defined contribution (401k-style) |

## FERS — The Three-Legged Stool

FERS is a **three-part retirement system** created by the Federal Employees' Retirement System Act of 1986 (P.L. 99-335), effective January 1, 1987. It replaced CSRS for all new federal employees and was designed to work alongside Social Security.

* see also: <a href="https://www.opm.gov/retirement-center/fers-information/">OPM — FERS Information</a>
* see also: <a href="./dc-federal-formulas#fers-formulas">FERS Pension Formulas & Worked Examples</a>

### The Three Components

| Component | Type | Who Contributes | Portable? |
|-----------|------|----------------|-----------|
| **FERS Basic Annuity** | Defined benefit (pension) | Employee + agency | No — refund of contributions only if you leave |
| **Social Security** | Social insurance | Employee (6.2%) + agency (6.2%) | Yes |
| **Thrift Savings Plan (TSP)** | Defined contribution (401k-style) | Employee (voluntary) + agency (automatic 1% + up to 4% match) | Yes |

### FERS Employee Contribution Rates

Congress has increased FERS employee contributions over time, creating three tiers:

| Tier | Applies To | Employee Contribution | Created By |
|------|-----------|----------------------|------------|
| **FERS** (Original) | Hired before 1/1/2013 | 0.8% of basic pay | FERS Act of 1986 |
| **FERS-RAE** (Revised Annuity Employee) | Hired 1/1/2013 – 12/31/2013 | 3.1% of basic pay | <a href="https://www.govexec.com/pay-benefits/2026/03/how-federal-retirement-benefits-have-changed-over-years/412244/">Middle Class Tax Relief and Job Creation Act of 2012 (P.L. 112-96)</a> |
| **FERS-FRAE** (Further Revised Annuity Employee) | Hired on/after 1/1/2014 | 4.4% of basic pay | <a href="https://www.secretservice.gov/employee-support/benefits/retirement-fers">Bipartisan Budget Act of 2013</a> |

All three tiers receive the **same pension benefit formula** — the only difference is how much you contribute. Special-category employees (law enforcement officers, firefighters, air traffic controllers) pay an additional 0.5% above their tier rate.

* see also: <a href="https://stwserve.com/high-three-average-salary-calculation-2024/">Serving Those Who Serve — High-3 Average Salary Calculation</a>

| Pathway | Age Requirement | Service Requirement | Annuity Reduction? |

## CSRS — The Legacy System

The <a href="https://www.opm.gov/retirement-center/csrs-information/">Civil Service Retirement System</a> was established in 1920 and is the oldest federal retirement system. It is a standalone defined benefit plan — CSRS employees generally do **not** participate in Social Security and do **not** receive TSP employer matching. CSRS was closed to new entrants effective January 1, 1984. As of FY2022, only about 2% of the active federal workforce remained under CSRS.

CSRS employees contribute 7% of their salary (7.5% for congressional employees, 8% for certain law enforcement). The CSRS formula uses a weighted three-tier multiplier (1.5%/1.75%/2.0%) with a maximum benefit of 80% of high-3.

## Foreign Service Retirement Systems

The Foreign Service operates its own retirement systems, parallel to CSRS and FERS but governed by the <a href="https://fam.state.gov/fam/03fam/03fam6110.html">Foreign Service Act of 1980 (P.L. 96-465)</a>:

- **FSRDS** (Foreign Service Retirement & Disability System) — the CSRS equivalent for FSOs hired before 1984. Participants contribute 7.25% and receive a 2.0% per year formula. Closed to new entrants.
- **FSPS** (Foreign Service Pension System) — the FERS equivalent for FSOs hired after 12/31/1983. Participants contribute 1.35%, receive Social Security and TSP matching, and benefit from an enhanced formula (1.7% for first 20 years) reflecting the demanding nature of Foreign Service careers.

Both systems cover employees across State Department, USAID, USAGM, Commerce, Peace Corps, and the Foreign Service Corps at USDA.

## Thrift Savings Plan (TSP)

The TSP is the federal government's defined contribution retirement savings plan, similar to a private-sector 401(k). It is available to all FERS and CSRS employees, as well as uniformed military members.

| Feature | FERS Employees | CSRS Employees |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Agency automatic contribution | 1% of basic pay (regardless of employee contribution) | None |
| Agency matching | Dollar-for-dollar on first 3% + 50 cents on next 2% = up to 5% total | None |
| Employee contribution limit (2026) | <a href="https://www.tsp.gov">IRS annual limit</a> (see TSP website for current year) | Same limit, but no match |
| Catch-up contributions (age 50+) | Additional amount above annual limit | Same |
| Vesting (agency contributions) | 3 years for automatic 1%; matching is immediately vested | N/A |

TSP offers six core investment funds: G Fund (government securities), F Fund (bonds), C Fund (S&P 500), S Fund (small/mid-cap), I Fund (international), and L Funds (lifecycle/target-date).

* see also: <a href="https://www.tsp.gov">TSP.gov — Official Thrift Savings Plan Website</a>

## D.C. Local Government Retirement Systems

The District of Columbia operates its own retirement systems for local government employees, entirely separate from the federal systems. The structure is unique because of the 1997 Balanced Budget Act, which split pension obligations between the federal government and D.C.

### The DCRB Systems

The <a href="https://dcrb.dc.gov/">District of Columbia Retirement Board (DCRB)</a> administers two defined benefit pension plans:

| Plan | Covers | Administered By |
|------|--------|----------------|
| **D.C. Police Officers' and Firefighters' Retirement Plan** | MPD officers, FEMS firefighters/EMTs | <a href="https://dcrb.dc.gov/service/retirement">DCRB</a> |
| **D.C. Teachers' Retirement Plan** | DCPS teachers (ET-classified employees) | <a href="https://dcrb.dc.gov/service/retirement">DCRB</a> |
| **D.C. Judges' Retirement Plan** | D.C. Superior Court and Court of Appeals judges | <a href="https://home.treasury.gov/about/offices/management/human-resources-and-chief-human-capital-officer/dc-pensions">U.S. Treasury</a> |

A separate **401(a) Defined Contribution Plan** covers D.C. general government employees (non-teacher, non-safety) hired on or after October 1, 1987. These employees receive a <a href="https://cfo.dc.gov/service/dc-government-retirement-plans">5% employer contribution</a> (5.5% for corrections officers) into a defined contribution account — they do **not** have a traditional pension.

D.C. government employees hired **before** October 1, 1987 (who are not teachers, police, or firefighters) participate in CSRS.

* see also: <a href="./dc-federal-formulas#dc-local-pension-formulas">D.C. Local Pension Formulas & Worked Examples</a>
* see also: <a href="https://cfo.dc.gov/service/dc-government-retirement-plans">OCFO — D.C. Government Retirement Plans</a>
* see also: <a href="https://dchr.dc.gov/page/employee-retirement">DCHR — Employee Retirement</a>

### The 1997 Split: Frozen Plan vs. Replacement Plan

Under the <a href="https://home.treasury.gov/about/offices/management/human-resources-and-chief-human-capital-officer/dc-pensions">National Capital Revitalization and Self-Government Improvement Act of 1997</a> (Balanced Budget Act, P.L. 105-33):

- **Pre-July 1, 1997 benefits ("Frozen Plan"):** The **federal government** (U.S. Treasury) assumed responsibility for all pension benefits earned by D.C. teachers, police, firefighters, and judges for service through June 30, 1997. This included the District's massive unfunded liability.
- **Post-June 30, 1997 benefits ("Replacement Plan"):** The **District of Columbia government** is responsible for all benefits earned after that date.

DCRB serves as the benefits administrator for both portions, but funding comes from two separate sources.

* see also: <a href="https://cfo.dc.gov/publication/2023-dy0-dc-retirement-board">OCFO — DCRB Budget Overview</a>

## Federal Labor Relations & Bargaining Units

Federal labor relations operate under a fundamentally different framework than state and local bargaining. Understanding this is critical because, unlike California or New York, **federal unions cannot bargain over pay, benefits, or pension formulas** — those are set by Congress.

### The Legal Framework

Federal collective bargaining is governed by the <a href="https://www.fedelaw.com/collective-bargaining-federal-employees/">Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute</a> (Title 5 U.S.C. Chapter 71), enacted as part of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978. This codified rights that President John F. Kennedy first granted via Executive Order 10988 in 1962.

Key differences from state/local bargaining:

| Feature | Federal | State/Local (e.g., California, New York) |
|---------|---------|------------------------------------------|
| **Pay** | Set by Congress (GS schedule, locality pay) — **not negotiable** | Negotiated through MOUs/contracts |
| **Pension formulas** | Set by federal statute — **not negotiable** | Often negotiable (e.g., CalPERS contract amendments) |
| **Health insurance** | FEHB program set by OPM — **not negotiable** | Often negotiated |
| **What CAN be bargained** | Working conditions, telework, schedules, grievance procedures, disciplinary procedures, safety | All of the above plus pay and benefits |
| **Right to strike** | **Prohibited** by law | Varies by state |
| **Impasse resolution** | <a href="https://www.federaltimes.com/management/hr/2021/09/01/answers-to-federal-workers-questions-about-government-labor-unions/">Federal Services Impasses Panel (FSIP)</a> | Varies — mediation, arbitration, fact-finding |
| **Oversight body** | <a href="https://www.flra.gov">Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA)</a> | State PERBs/labor boards |

* see also: <a href="https://www.fedelaw.com/collective-bargaining-federal-employees/">The Law Office of Justin Schnitzer — Federal Collective Bargaining</a>
* see also: <a href="https://www.federaltimes.com/management/hr/2021/09/01/answers-to-federal-workers-questions-about-government-labor-unions/">Federal Times — Answers to Federal Workers' Questions About Unions</a>

### How Bargaining Units Work at the Federal Level

More than half of federal jobs are classified as **bargaining unit positions**, meaning a union has been certified as the exclusive representative for that group of employees. Key mechanics:

1. **Unit determination:** The <a href="https://www.flra.gov">FLRA</a> determines which employees share a "clear and identifiable community of interest" and are eligible to form a bargaining unit. A bargaining unit is only ever covered by one union.

2. **Exclusive representation:** Once certified, the union represents **all** employees in the unit — whether or not they are dues-paying members. Federal law prohibits closed shops (mandatory union membership).

3. **Collective bargaining agreements (CBAs):** Unions negotiate CBAs with agencies covering working conditions, telework policies, disciplinary procedures, grievance processes, safety measures, and how much "official time" union reps can use during work hours.

4. **No MOUs for pay/benefits:** Unlike California's state bargaining units that negotiate MOUs covering salary and pension formula changes, federal unions cannot negotiate pay or pension changes. Those require acts of Congress. Federal union lobbyists instead spend much of their time advocating on Capitol Hill for pay raises, benefit improvements, and protective legislation.

5. **Official time:** Union representatives negotiate, represent employees, and perform union duties on paid government time — a concept frequently debated in political circles.

### How Federal Pension Formulas Are Determined

Unlike state and local systems where pension formulas can be changed through bargaining and contract amendments, **all federal pension formulas are established by federal statute** and can only be changed by Congress:

| System | Governing Law |
|--------|--------------|
| FERS | <a href="https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47084">Federal Employees' Retirement System Act of 1986 (P.L. 99-335)</a>, as amended |
| CSRS | <a href="https://www.opm.gov/retirement-center/csrs-information/">Civil Service Retirement Act of 1920</a>, as amended |
| FSRDS/FSPS | <a href="https://fam.state.gov/fam/03fam/03fam6110.html">Foreign Service Act of 1980 (P.L. 96-465)</a> |
| FERS-RAE contributions | Middle Class Tax Relief and Job Creation Act of 2012 (P.L. 112-96) |
| FERS-FRAE contributions | Bipartisan Budget Act of 2013 |
| D.C. Teachers | <a href="https://dcrb.dc.gov/page/summary-plan-faqs-retired-teachers">D.C. Code §§ 38-2021.01 et seq.</a> |
| D.C. Police/Fire | <a href="https://dcrb.dc.gov/page/summary-plans-faqs-retired-police-and-firefighters">D.C. Code §§ 5-701 to 5-762</a> |

This means federal unions influence pension policy through **legislative advocacy**, not direct bargaining. Organizations like <a href="https://www.narfe.org">NARFE</a> (National Active and Retired Federal Employees Association), AFGE, and NTEU lobby Congress on bills like the Equal COLA Act, the Federal Retirement Fairness Act, and proposals to change contribution rates.

* see also: <a href="https://febabenefits.org/blog/role-of-unions-federal-employment/">FEBA — The Role of Unions in Federal Employment</a>

### 2025–2026: Federal Labor Relations Under Pressure

The federal labor landscape has been significantly disrupted by Executive Order 14251 (March 2025), which invoked the national security exemption under 5 U.S.C. § 7103(b)(1) to remove collective bargaining rights from employees at over 40 federal agencies and subdivisions. This triggered a wave of lawsuits from AFGE, NTEU, AFSA, and other unions. As of early 2026, courts have issued mixed rulings — with some initial injunctions being vacated on appeal — and the situation remains in active litigation.

### Who Determines D.C. Local Pension Benefits?

D.C. local pension benefits occupy a unique middle ground — governed by D.C. Code (enacted by the D.C. Council) but with a federal backstop:

| Element | Who Decides | Authority |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| D.C. pension formulas (teachers, police, fire) | D.C. Council | <a href="https://dcrb.dc.gov/page/summary-plan-faqs-retired-teachers">D.C. Code Title 38 (teachers) and Title 5 (police/fire)</a> |
| Pre-1997 benefit obligations | U.S. Congress (via 1997 Balanced Budget Act) | <a href="https://home.treasury.gov/about/offices/management/human-resources-and-chief-human-capital-officer/dc-pensions">P.L. 105-33, Title XI</a> |
| Employer contribution rates | DCRB actuarial determination | <a href="https://cfo.dc.gov/publication/2025-fd0-police-officers-fire-fighters-retirement-system">D.C. Code §1-907.03</a> |
| D.C. general government 401(a) plan | D.C. Council | D.C. Code |
| Changes affecting pre-1997 benefits | **Both** D.C. Council **and** U.S. Congress | <a href="https://home.treasury.gov/about/offices/management/human-resources-and-chief-human-capital-officer/dc-pensions">Legislation after 6/29/1997 does not affect federal benefits unless Congress also acts</a> |

D.C. unions (FOP for police, IAFF Local 36 for firefighters, WTU for teachers) can negotiate working conditions through collective bargaining, but pension formula changes require action by the D.C. Council — similar to the New York model where benefits are set by legislation rather than direct bargaining.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **FERS** | Federal Employees Retirement System — the three-part retirement system (pension + Social Security + TSP) covering most federal employees hired after 1983 |
| **CSRS** | Civil Service Retirement System — the standalone defined benefit pension for employees hired before 1984; closed to new entrants |
| **High-3** | The average of your highest 3 consecutive years of basic pay — the salary base for federal pension calculations |
| **TSP** | Thrift Savings Plan — the federal defined contribution (401k-style) retirement savings plan |
| **MRA** | Minimum Retirement Age — the earliest age (55–57) a FERS employee can retire with an immediate annuity |
| **VERA** | Voluntary Early Retirement Authority — allows agencies to offer early retirement during reorganizations |
| **VSIP** | Voluntary Separation Incentive Payment — a lump-sum buyout (up to $25,000) offered alongside VERA |
| **SRS** | Special Retirement Supplement — a bridge payment approximating Social Security for FERS retirees who retire before 62 |
| **Diet COLA** | The reduced COLA that FERS annuitants receive when inflation exceeds 2% |
| **DCRB** | District of Columbia Retirement Board — administers the D.C. Teachers' and Police/Fire retirement plans |
| **FLRA** | Federal Labor Relations Authority — the independent agency that oversees federal sector labor-management relations |
| **FSIP** | Federal Services Impasses Panel — resolves bargaining impasses between agencies and unions |
| **Official Time** | Paid work time that union representatives use to conduct representational activities |
| **CBA** | Collective Bargaining Agreement — the contract between a federal agency and the union representing its employees |
| **FERS-RAE** | FERS Revised Annuity Employee — hired in 2013, pays 3.1% contribution |
| **FERS-FRAE** | FERS Further Revised Annuity Employee — hired in 2014+, pays 4.4% contribution |
